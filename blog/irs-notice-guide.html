What to Do If You Receive an IRS Notice

Your step-by-step guide to understanding and responding to IRS correspondence

Don't Panic – You Have Options

Receiving a notice from the IRS can be stressful and confusing. However, it's important to understand that an IRS notice doesn't automatically mean you're in serious trouble. The IRS sends millions of notices each year for various reasons, from simple clarifications to more complex tax adjustments. The key is to respond promptly and appropriately.

Step 1: Read Your Notice Carefully

The first step is to carefully read the entire notice. Look for:

  • Notice Number:Different numbers indicate different types of issues (CP notices are common)
  • Tax Year(s) in Question:Identifies which year's return is being addressed
  • Reason for the Notice:Explains why the IRS is contacting you
  • Amount Owed (if any):The dollar amount under question
  • Deadline for Response:Critical – missing this can have serious consequences
  • Contact Information:IRS representative's name and phone number

Common Types of IRS Notices

CP14 or CP501 – Payment Reminder

You missed a payment or underpaid taxes. If you believe this is an error or can pay immediately, respond within 30 days.

CP11 – Proposed Changes to Your Return

The IRS has proposed adjustments to your return. You have the opportunity to respond with documentation supporting your original return.

CP515 – Return Was Filed Late

Your return was filed after the deadline. The IRS may assess failure-to-file penalties that could be reduced with reasonable cause.

Audit Notice (CP2000, CP504, etc.)

The IRS is conducting an examination of your return. This requires careful documentation and may benefit from professional representation.

Collection Actions

Notices about liens, levies, or wage garnishment indicate the IRS is taking collection action. These require immediate attention.

Step 2: Don't Ignore It

This cannot be stressed enough: ignoring an IRS notice is one of the worst things you can do. Failure to respond can result in:

  • Increased penalties and interest charges
  • Wage garnishment (levy)
  • Bank account seizures
  • Tax liens on your property
  • Loss of business licenses
  • Legal judgment against you

Step 3: Gather Your Documentation

Collect all relevant documents related to the issue:

  • Original tax return and any amendments
  • Receipts and invoices for deductions claimed
  • Bank statements and financial records
  • Correspondence with the IRS
  • Any written explanations you want to provide

Step 4: Respond Within the Deadline

The deadline stated in your notice is binding. You typically have 30 days, but some notices allow longer. Your response options include:

  • Pay the Amount:If you agree with the assessment
  • Request a Collections Appeal:If you dispute the amount
  • Request an Appeals Conference:For certain disputes
  • Submit Documentation:To support your position
  • Request Professional Representation:Have a tax professional respond on your behalf

Step 5: Consider Professional Help

While you can respond yourself, having a qualified tax professional or Enrolled Agent represent you offers several advantages:

  • They understand IRS procedures and protocols
  • They can communicate directly with the IRS on your behalf
  • They have experience resolving similar situations
  • They ensure nothing is overlooked or missed
  • They may help reduce penalties through penalty abatement requests

Important: Appeals and Your Rights

If you disagree with the IRS's determination, you have the right to appeal. The notice should include instructions on how to request an appeals conference. This is a formal process where you can present your case to an independent IRS Appeals Officer.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Missing the deadline:Always respond by the date specified
  • Not keeping copies:Keep copies of everything you submit
  • Not responding in writing:Put your response in writing with a paper trail
  • Sending cash:Always pay by check or money order with your tax ID
  • Being unclear:Be specific and organized in your response
  • Hiding information:Full transparency is always better than omissions
