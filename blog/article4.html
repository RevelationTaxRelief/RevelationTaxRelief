Offer in Compromise: A Structured Approach to Tax Debt

Published: November 16, 2025

An Offer in Compromise (OIC) is an agreement between a taxpayer and the IRS that settles a tax liability for less than the full amount owed. It's a powerful tool for taxpayers facing significant financial hardship, but it's not a simple process and requires a structured, meticulous approach to be successful.

When is an OIC Considered?

The IRS may consider an OIC based on three primary grounds:

  • Doubt as to Collectibility: This is the most common reason. The IRS believes you cannot pay your full tax liability. They assess your ability to pay by looking at your income, expenses, equity in assets, and future earning potential.
  • Doubt as to Liability: There's a genuine doubt that the assessed tax liability is correct. This is rare once the tax has been assessed.
  • Effective Tax Administration: Although there's no doubt the tax is correct and collectible, requiring full payment would cause economic hardship or would be unfair and inequitable.

The OIC Application Process

The OIC process is rigorous and requires extensive financial disclosure. Key steps include:

  1. Eligibility Check: Ensure all tax returns are filed and current tax payments are being made.
  2. Financial Analysis: Complete Form 433-A (OIC) for individuals or Form 433-B (OIC) for businesses, detailing all assets, liabilities, income, and expenses.
  3. Proposal Submission: Submit a formal offer amount, along with the application fee and initial payment.
  4. IRS Review: The IRS evaluates your offer based on your ability to pay, income, expenses, and asset equity. This can take several months.
  5. Negotiation and Decision: The IRS may counter-offer, or accept/reject your proposal.
