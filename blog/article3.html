Tax Liens and Levies: What You Need to Know

Published: November 9, 2025

When tax debts go unpaid, the IRS and state tax authorities have powerful tools to collect what is owed. Among the most serious are tax liens and tax levies. Understanding the difference between these two and how to address them is crucial for protecting your financial well-being.

What is a Tax Lien?

A tax lien is a legal claim against your property (real estate, vehicles, financial assets) when you neglect or fail to pay a tax debt. It secures the government's interest in your property and establishes its priority over other creditors. A federal tax lien can attach to all your property and rights to property, even property acquired after the lien arises. It also negatively impacts your credit score and can make it difficult to sell or refinance property.

What is a Tax Levy?

A tax levy is a more aggressive enforcement action. While a lien is a claim against property, a levy actually seizes property to satisfy a tax debt. The IRS can levy your bank accounts, wages, retirement accounts, and even physical property. Before a levy can occur, the IRS must typically send several notices, including a Final Notice of Intent to Levy and Notice of Your Right to a Hearing.

Addressing Liens and Levies

If you are facing a tax lien or levy, immediate action is essential. Options for resolution include:

  • Paying the Tax Debt in Full: The most straightforward way to release a lien or stop a levy.
  • Installment Agreement: Establishing a payment plan can often lead to the release of a levy and sometimes a lien.
  • Offer in Compromise (OIC): If accepted, an OIC can resolve your tax debt for a lesser amount, leading to lien release.
  • Withdrawal of Lien: In certain circumstances, the IRS may withdraw a Notice of Federal Tax Lien, which removes it from public record.
  • Appeals: You have the right to appeal certain IRS collection actions.
